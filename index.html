The uses of and differences between the Present Perfect and Past Simple tenses

Intermediate British English lesson: clear explanations, examples with Polish names and places, a 16-question multiple-choice test, and a table of common irregular verbs.

How the Present Perfect functions

The Present Perfect (have/has + past participle) links the past with the present. We use it to talk about experiences, changes, completed actions with present relevance, and actions that happened at an unspecified time before now.

Examples
  • Have you ever been to Łódź? — Yes, I have been there twice.
  • She has just finished her homework.
  • They have lived in Kraków since 2018.

How the Past Simple functions

The Past Simple (verb in past form) describes actions that started and finished at a definite time in the past. The time can be stated directly or implied by time expressions.

Examples
  • He visited Gdańsk last summer.
  • Maria called me yesterday.
  • We moved to Warszawa in 2010.

Key differences between Present Perfect and Past Simple

1. Time reference

Use Past Simple when you mention a finished time in the past (yesterday, last year, in 2015, when I was a child). Use Present Perfect when the exact time is not given and the action has relevance now.

2. Time flags (typical with Past Simple)

  • yesterday; last night/week/year; in 1999; two days ago; when I was a child.

3. Present Perfect vocabulary (typical words)

  • ever, never, just, already, yet, recently, so far, since, for, lately.

4. Use for life experience vs. finished events

Present Perfect expresses life experiences without a time: "I have visited Poznań." Past Simple gives the finished event with time: "I visited Poznań in 2016."

5. Present relevance and result

Present Perfect emphasises the present result: "I have broken my arm" (so now it affects me). Past Simple states it happened in the past: "I broke my arm when I was a child."

Multiple-choice test — 16 questions

Choose the correct option. Answers are scrambled in order for each question.

1. Have you ___ to Warsaw before?
2. They ___ to Gdańsk last summer.
3. I ___ already ___ my homework.
4. When ___ you move to Łódź?
5. She ___ just ___ her coffee.
6. We ___ in Poznań in 2012.
7. I can't find my keys. I ___ them.
8. They ___ already ___ a new flat last month.
9. ___ you ever seen the view from the Palace of Culture in Warszawa?
10. Last year, Marta ___ a painting course in Kraków.
11. I ___ never ___ sushi before I visited Gdańsk.
12. They ___ already left when we arrived.
13. How long ___ you lived in Warszawa?
14. I ___ my phone yesterday.
15. She ___ in Poznań since 2015.
16. I ___ to Łódź last month.
Answer key and brief notes
1. a — Present Perfect for experience.
2. a — Past Simple with a finished time (last summer).
3. a — have + past participle; already is typical with Present Perfect.
4. a — Past Simple (specific time expected).
5. a — has just drunk (just + Present Perfect).
6. a — lived (in 2012 — finished past).
7. a — have lost (present relevance; result now).
8. c — bought already last month (Past Simple; though "have bought" conflicts with "last month").
9. a — Have (Have you ever seen...?).
10. a — attended (Last year — Past Simple).
11. a — had tried (before I visited — Past Perfect fits sequence) *Note: "had tried" emphasises sequence; "had never tried" is correct here.*
12. a — had (They had already left when we arrived — Past Perfect).
13. a — have (How long have you lived... — duration to present).
14. a — lost (yesterday — Past Simple).
15. a — has lived (since 2015 — Present Perfect with since).
16. a — went (last month — Past Simple).
